# Development Note: Full Jurisdictional Pathway Constraint Matrix
## Background for Chapter 15, Section 5 (Trust Across the Six Fields)

**Context:** Section 5 claims that the omitted combinations in the three-constraint jurisdictional table "tend to collapse toward one of these four dominant structures over time." This document provides the full derivation supporting that claim.

---

## The Three Binary Variables

The Jurisdictional field's validation pathway is evaluated along three orthogonal constraints:

- **Open (O):** Can affected participants access the validation pathway?
- **Binding (B):** Do signals that enter the pathway actually constrain enforcement outcomes?
- **Legible (L):** Can participants understand how the pathway functions?

Three binary variables generate eight possible states (2³ = 8). The chapter presents four dominant states. This document analyzes all eight, derives the structural dynamics of the four intermediate states, and demonstrates why the dominant states function as attractors.

## Dominant States: Why They Are Attractors

The four dominant states are structurally coherent: they do not contain internal contradictions that generate their own correction pressure.

**State 1: Pure domination (O=No, B=No, L=No)**
Makes no claim to provide what it withholds. Participants have no formal expectation of access, consequence, or comprehension. No differential Trust Debt accumulates because no promise is made. Stable under the constraint that coercive capacity remains sufficient to suppress exit. The attractor condition: domination that attempts to provide one of the three properties without the others destabilizes itself by creating visible contradictions between claim and reality.

**State 6: Symbolic participation (O=Yes, B=No, L=Yes)**
Provides enough performance of legitimacy to absorb correction pressure without resolving it. Participants can see and use the pathway but cannot compel consequence. The formal openness and legibility distribute the perception of agency without distributing actual enforcement power. Stable because the correction pressure is defused through the performance before it can accumulate into organized challenge. Participants who observe the gap between participation and consequence are told (and can see) that the process exists; the legitimacy of the complaint is absorbed into the process that produced the result.

**State 3: Arbitrary coercion (O=No, B=Yes, L=No)**
Enforcement without accessibility or comprehension faces no organized internal challenge because participants cannot navigate to challenge it and cannot see clearly enough to coordinate around it. Stable under the constraint that the binding enforcement capacity remains intact. The attractor condition: coercion that adds legibility without openness (State 7) becomes visible in its inaccessibility, generating political pressure that arbitrary coercion without legibility avoids.

**State 8: Functional governance (O=Yes, B=Yes, L=Yes)**
All three constraints are satisfied simultaneously. No differential Trust Debt accumulates between population layers. The Validation chain can close cleanly at Measurement and Calibration because signals enter, produce consequences, and can be understood by those affected. The attractor condition: any degradation of one of the three properties moves the system toward one of the intermediate states and generates pressure that either restores the degraded property or drives the system toward a different dominant attractor.

## Intermediate States: Collapse Dynamics

**State 2: Empty openness (O=Yes, B=No, L=No)**
The pathway is formally accessible but produces no consequence and cannot be understood. Performative openness at its most minimal.

*Instability mechanism:* Without legibility, openness cannot even produce symbolic legitimacy, because participants cannot see enough to believe the performance. Without binding, the openness produces no result. The combination provides neither real nor apparent validation function. Because the system makes a claim (openness) that contradicts its performance (no consequence, no comprehension), it generates Trust Debt through the gap between claim and reality.

*Collapse direction:* Toward **Pure domination** as the hollow openness is abandoned or formally removed, OR toward **Symbolic participation** if legibility is added to give the performance enough form to function as legitimacy theater. The direction is determined by whether the system's controllers find more value in maintaining the form of openness or in dropping the pretense.

**State 3: Blind coercion (O=No, B=Yes, L=No)**
Enforcement is real and produces consequences, but neither access nor comprehension is available.

*Instability mechanism:* Because binding is genuine, outcomes are produced, but those outcomes cannot be anticipated, navigated toward, or challenged by affected participants. The system generates real consequences without a comprehensible relationship between behavior and outcome. This is structurally identical to Pure domination from the participant's perspective (they cannot access or understand the pathway), but the binding property means the system is actually doing something rather than simply asserting authority. The contradiction: genuine enforcement without navigable structure produces compliance through fear rather than through understanding, which is less stable over time than either fully comprehensible governance or pure domination.

*Collapse direction:* Toward **Pure domination** if binding weakens and the system reduces to assertion without consequence, OR toward **Arbitrary coercion** (the chapter's dominant State 3) which is stable precisely because it does not attempt to provide the inaccessible legibility that State 3 here is missing. Blind coercion that adds legibility without adding openness moves to State 7 (Exclusive governance) on its way to either functional governance or the chapter's arbitrary coercion state.

**State 4: Functional but opaque (O=Yes, B=Yes, L=No)**
The pathway is accessible and produces real consequences, but participants cannot understand how it works.

*Instability mechanism:* Legibility absence means the binding property is not equally distributed across the participant population. Those with access to specialized intermediaries (lawyers, advocates, technical experts) can navigate the pathway and experience something close to functional governance. Those without such access are effectively excluded despite formal openness, experiencing something closer to arbitrary coercion. The system therefore applies different structural states to different populations simultaneously. This differential creates internal pressure because it generates differential Trust Debt: participants who cannot navigate the opaque pathway observe others successfully doing so, which makes the inaccessibility legible even though the pathway itself is not.

*Collapse direction:* Toward **Functional governance** if legibility reforms succeed and the population able to navigate the pathway expands toward all affected participants. OR toward **Arbitrary coercion** for the majority if opacity becomes entrenched and capture by the specialist class deepens. The two dominant attractors can coexist for different population segments until one wins: either legibility spreads outward or effective access contracts inward.

**State 5: Transparent futility (O=No, B=No, L=Yes)**
Rules and procedures are documented and clear, but access is restricted and outcomes do not follow from the stated procedures.

*Instability mechanism:* This is the most sophisticated form of domination because it uses legibility as a display of power rather than as a navigation tool. Participants can read exactly how the system is supposed to work and observe that it does not work that way for them. The legibility makes the gap between stated and actual function visible, which is itself a form of Trust Debt display. The system communicates: "you can see the pathway; you cannot reach it; reaching it would not matter." This generates a specific kind of pressure: not ignorant frustration (which is the response to pure domination) but informed frustration (which is the response to visible injustice).

*Collapse direction:* Toward **Pure domination** if the legibility continues serving as demonstration of inaccessibility rather than navigation guidance, and the system removes legibility to reduce the visible contradiction. OR toward **Symbolic participation** if formal openness is added to give the legible structure something to perform with. The choice depends on whether maintaining legibility as display of power outweighs the pressure that legibility generates.

**State 7: Exclusive governance (O=No, B=Yes, L=Yes)**
The pathway is binding and comprehensible, but structurally restricted to a subset of affected participants.

*Instability mechanism:* This is the highest-tension intermediate state because legibility is present. Participants who cannot access the pathway can see clearly that it functions for others. The visible inaccessibility generates political pressure that the other forms of coercion avoid: affected participants know the mechanism, can observe it functioning for others, and understand what they are being denied. This generates organized correction pressure rather than diffuse frustration.

*Collapse direction:* Toward **Functional governance** if access expands to reach all affected participants. OR toward **Arbitrary coercion** (the chapter's dominant State 3) if legibility is progressively obscured to prevent the inaccessibility from being visible, removing the political pressure by removing the ability to see and compare. Legibility without openness is politically unstable in ways that pure domination is not, because it shows participants the mechanism they cannot reach and provides the cognitive tools to organize around that gap.

## Why the Four Dominant States Are Attractors: Structural Summary

Each intermediate state contains at least one structural contradiction between what it claims and what it delivers. These contradictions generate internal pressure toward resolution because:

**1. Differential Trust Debt.** Intermediate states typically produce different effective outcomes for different population segments simultaneously. This differential is itself observable and generates correction pressure from those in the worse-served segment.

**2. Lifecycle incompleteness.** The Trust Validation chain (Declaration → Activation → Measurement → Calibration) requires all three pathway properties to close cleanly. A system missing one property cannot complete Calibration accurately, and the incomplete calibration accumulates as misalignment between the system's stated and actual function.

**3. Cross-field misalignment.** Trust systems require coherence across all six fields. A configuration that is internally inconsistent generates cross-field pressure: the Tribal field responds to the gap between expectation and outcome, the Cultural field responds to the gap between stated and observed legitimacy, the Economic field responds to the asymmetric enforcement costs.

**4. Attractor stability condition.** The dominant states are stable because they do not make claims that generate contradictions between their structure and their function. Pure domination makes no promise. Symbolic participation promises process, not outcome, and delivers process. Arbitrary coercion promises enforcement and delivers it, making no claim to accessibility or comprehension. Functional governance delivers all three.

The intermediate states make partial claims that are structurally unsustainable: they promise one or two properties while structurally failing to deliver the third, and this gap between claim and delivery is the source of the pressure that drives them toward resolution.

## Application to Chapter Text

The sentence added to Section 5 ("The omitted combinations are transitional or unstable states that tend to collapse toward one of these four dominant structures over time") is therefore derivable rather than merely claimed. The collapse directions for each intermediate state are predictable from the structural dynamics, and the direction of collapse depends on the internal incentives of the system's controlling actors and the accumulated pressure from affected participants.

This derivation also reinforces the full suffrage requirement: full suffrage (State 8, functional governance) is the only state that is simultaneously stable and genuinely trust-generating. The other three dominant attractors are stable either through suppressing the validation pathway (States 1 and 3) or through providing a performance of the pathway without its substance (State 6). Only State 8 produces actual Trust compounding rather than Trust Debt accumulation or Trust Debt concealment.
